ENRC rejects offer from co-founders

By Lina Saigol and Anousha Sakoui

The independent board of Eurasian Natural Resources Corp has rejected a proposed offer by the embattled London-listed miner’s billionaire founding shareholders to take it private.

Alexander Mashkevich, Alijan Ibragimov and Pathokh Chodiev made their offer on Friday, comprising 175p in cash and 0.231 of an existing Kazakhmys share for each ENRC share and valuing the group at just under $5bn.

That was rejected by ENRC’s seven-strong independent committee on Monday on the grounds that it “materially undervalued” the FTSE 100 miner.

At the time the proposal was presented, it referenced a Kazakhmys share price of 370p a share, which together with the 175p a share in cash represented a total price of 260p for each ENRC share.

The offer represents a premium of about 7.3 per cent to ENRC’s closing price of 230p on April 18, the day before the bidding group announced it may make an offer.

However on a 30-trading day average before the announcement, ENRC shares were valued at 277p, which with a standard 30 per cent premium in a takeover would yield 360p.

Because the Kazakh government is tendering its shares in Kazakhmys – which itself holds a 26 per cent stake in ENRC – as part of the consortium offer, the UK Takeover Panel ruled that Kazkhmys should be treated as a party to the indicative offer.

Shares in ENRC, which listed on the London Stock Exchange at 540p in December 2007, were down 2.9 per cent at 263.8p, valuing the company’s equity at £3.5bn.

Following the offer, Kazakhmys itself clarified that it was not part of the consortium, no new shares were being issued and that its board would “give due consideration to any proposal made by the consortium to shareholders at the appropriate time.”

The Panel on Friday agreed to extend the deadline by which ENRC must either improve its offer or walk away to June 3.

The potential buyout comes amid an investigation by the Serious Fraud Office over allegations of fraud, bribery and corruption relating to ENRC’s activities in Kazakhstan and Africa.

Felix Vulis, ENRC’s chief executive, has said the company is “co-operating fully” with the investigation.
